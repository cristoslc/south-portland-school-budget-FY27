Chair DeAngelis shared her support for the South Portland City Council’s Resolve # 5–25/26. There will be an

Angela Kabisa shared a number of updates from South Portland schools.
Report of the Superintendent
Dr. Entwistle began his report by recognizing National School Counseling Week, which was February 2-6, 2026.
Rebecca Stern, SPMS Principal and Liana Popkin, Community School Director, shared a report on the community
school model, which integrates academics with health, social and community services to reduce barriers and support
all students. An expansion of this strategy would be supported by an education foundation.
Dr. Jo Prince, Assistant Superintendent, reported on themes from the Budget Forums. About 100 people participated
over two nights and shared their value in public education, but are grappling with the challenges ahead (declining
enrollment, rising costs, and reasonable taxes.) Folks shared their appreciation for the dialogue and opportunity in

BOARD OF EDUCATION
the forum, their desire for transparency and community input throughout the budget process, and a goal of protecting
what makes our schools strong.
Dr. Jo Prince also shared an update regarding Pre-K and Child Developmental Services (CDS). The Pre-K lottery is
open for the 2026-27 school year. South Portland has entered Cohort 3 to begin the transition for CDS. The district’s
partnership with Chickadee will continue for Pre-K, and there are expansion talks with United for Childcare and
Headstart/The Opportunity Alliance.
Mike Nalli, Director of Operations and Safety, and Todd Livingston, Athletic Director, provided an update on the
Athletic Complex. The referendum approved will include a new eight-lane track, a new athletic field with natural
grass, new lights, and permanent restrooms. Pending weather conditions, Activitas will survey the current facility in
April 2026 and the design will be completed in June 2026. Permitting will start in July 2026 and bid documents
would go out in December 2026. Construction would start in April 2027 with the goal for this project to be
completed by September 1, 2027.
Dr. Entwistle reported on student enrollment, which is 2,748 (as of 2/3/26, +12 students since January 2026)

Workshop
Chair DeAngelis answered some of the public’s questions regarding the budget and the process.She clarified that $7
million is the amount that the Board must cut in the budget to bring a tax increase of 6% to City Council and the
voters.
Dr. Entwistle provided an update regarding storm days and remote learning for the remainder of the 2025-26 school
year.
Dr. Entwistle provided an overview that this is the first of four Board Workshops in March that will provide time for
the Board to work with district leadership to evaluate proposals that will impact the budget.
Small Principal Lori Pacholski introduced the presentation for potential options related to the elementary schools
and the FY27 budget.
SPHS Principal Sarah Glenn introduced that demographics of the five current elementary schools will be shared.
SPMS Principal Rebecca Stern reported that she experienced the transition of closing Memorial and Mahoney
Middle Schools and the opening of the new middle school, so she can share the lessons learned and offer support
with any elementary school changes.
Skillin Principal Bethany Connolly shared a high level budget overview focused on the elementary schools. The five
elementary schools make up 37.4% of the total budget at $31,390,000.
Kaler Principal Myra Caron reiterated that this meeting will focus on the elementary schools to provide multiple
models and options for how they might be able to reduce costs. Other areas of the budget will be addressed at
upcoming meetings.
Brown Principal Beth Kellogg shared current attendance zones and changes in enrollment over the past few years.
Dyer Principal Allison Hopkins shared that moving towards schools that more closely reflect the diversity of our
overall student body will benefit all learners.
Director of Multilingual Programs April Perkins shared that one of the district’s greatest assets is our linguistic and
cultural diversity. Reevaluating the structure of the elementary schools is a key step to empowering all South
Portland students to expand critical skills for communication, flexibility, empathy, and problem solving.
Assistant Director of Special Education Sonia Croft provided information about current students' services. The
district currently serves 618 students as of October 1, 2025. Beginning September 1, 2026, the district will assume
the responsibility of serving 4-year old students who are eligible for special education services, which is anticipated

to be an additional 80-90 children, bringing the total count to around 700 from PreK-12. The following year will
expand services for 3-year olds.
Skillin Assistant Principal Nikki Remsen shared the current average class size for each of the schools.
Director of Curriculum, Instruction, and Assessment Ellyn Feerick shared that the district has made efforts to ensure
that all students receive a common curriculum across the five elementary schools, including the adaptation of
IntoMath in the fall of 2025.
Technology Director Andy Wallace shared some benefits in the area of technology and professional learning.
Director of Operations Mike Nalli shared the considerations of operational and maintenance costs associated with
these different options. Skillin will require an HVAC system replacement in the near future with an estimated cost of
approximately $3 million.
Director of Finance Abigail Ketchen presented the Board with nine different scenarios and preliminary estimates to
how the elementary school reconfigurations can impact the budget for the FY27 budget as well as over the next five
years through FY31.
Assistant Superintendent Dr. Jo Prince thanked the leadership team for their work and recognizes that these options
mean significant change for the school community. She provided answers to the Board’s questions.

Brown School continues to provide engaging and inclusive experiences for students. This
Friday, March 6th, Spark Dance will present an interactive program in the gym, promoting
participation and inclusivity. Last week, classrooms celebrated the 100th day of school with
creativity and enthusiasm, making the milestone fun and memorable for all. Additionally, our
Brown School Skates program concluded another successful year, providing equitable access
to winter sports while encouraging healthy, active participation.
Dyer has a busy March for students and families. We kicked off our Read A Thon Monday, 3/2,
with a whole school Stop, Drop, and Read. We also kicked it off as a community on March 5th
with a Books and Bites event. We had teacher a family guest readers, a funny book cover
challenge, bookmark making, and Ms. Jess from the public library was there!
Kaler had a wonderful all-school community meeting filled with joy and school spirit! Our
kindergarten students kicked things off by warmly greeting everyone and singing a song about
kindness while proudly wearing their “Kindness Capes.” We also launched Reading in March
with the theme “Kaler Readers Champion Kindness,” which will include many fun activities, such
as a reading-themed spirit week and a character parade. The celebration wrapped up with the
long-awaited 4th grade parade of floats on Sphere robots — a creative event that had been
postponed several times and was well worth the wait!
Skillin is "Marching into Reading" this month with literacy and reading activities and surprises
planned throughout the month. We are encouraging reading at home with tips for families and
are asking families and staff members to share pictures of what reading looks like for them at
home. These pictures will make a community bulletin board that will be on display on March
19th for our literacy and math night. The theme this year is "Learning is SNOW much fun!" We
are also using the month of March to refocus on our school-wide expectations of being
respectful, responsible, and kind (being safe is always a focus!). Each week, will focus on one
expectation that students will earn Skyhawk tickets for as part of a grade level competition. The
grade level with the most tickets at the end of the month will earn a special, whole grade reward!
And, to reinforce for our students that our school-wide expectations apply to ALL members of
the Skillin community, students will have tickets they can distribute to staff. We are excited for a
month full of reading, some friendly competition, and celebrations!
Small Elementary School had a Celebration of Learning Night for families on March 5th.
Students shared their final projects related to their Grade Level Interdisciplinary units. Families
also had the oppprtunity to particiapte in some hands-on STEM activites related to these units. It
was fantastic to see so many families participating in this celebration. We will be kicking of our
schoolwide Read-a-Thon on March 16 and can't wait to seem how many minutes our students
are able to collectively read from March 16th through March 27th. Our Buildings & Grounds
Club has been hard at work planning for how to spruce up our outdoor environment. They have
recently installed an outdoor xylophone that they made and are discussing other options for how
they can help to create a more engaging outdoor setting for all.

SPHS has had a wonderful few weeks. We want to first congratulate and thank our fantastic
BSU for hosting a celebratory Black History Month, culminating in a school wide assembly on
Friday 2/27. This past weeked, SPHS was host to the One Act Festival where our Drama Club
preformed thier one act, Hearts Like Planets. This is coming off of our first ever fine arts night
on March 4th, featuring the one act play, musical performaces, art displays and more! This
week, we look forward to kicking off our graduation season on Wednesday, March 11th, with our
senior family night, senior finanical fitness fair and senior graduation meeting. Tomorrow night
though, we will welocme the SPHS class of 2030, our current 8th graders, for step up night!

South Portland School Department
Temperature Standards for School Facilities
The South Portland School Department is committed to maintaining safe, healthy, and
comfortable learning and working environments for staff and students. These guidelines are
established in alignment with the Maine L.D. 1: An Act to Improve Public Health Regarding
Indoor Air Quality in Schools.
Optimal Indoor Environment: The Board believes that the optimal indoor environment for
teaching and learning is between a minimum of 65 degrees and a maximum of 80 degrees and
adopts this as the “temperature standard” for the District’s schools.
The facilities department will maintain updated procedures to assist schools in mitigating
temperature changes outside of the optimal environment.
Decision Thresholds:
When temperatures are predicted to be outside of the optimal environment, administrators (or
their delegates) shall monitor both indoor and outdoor temperatures.
● Outdoor Recess/Gym Class: School administrators should decide whether or not to
have outdoor recess or gym due to temperature concerns.
● Early Dismissal/School Cancellation: The Superintendent will determine if school is
canceled or dismissed early due to temperature concerns.
● Co-curricular Events: The Superintendent will determine if co-curricular events are
canceled due to temperature concerns.

Procedure for Exceeding the Maximum Comfort Zone
When temperatures in the building exceed the maximum comfort zone, staff will follow
these procedures when safe, allowable, and available (but not limited to):
● Opening windows
● Pulling down shades or blinds
● Using fans
● Opening doors
● Turning off heat-producing electronics/equipment
● Having water readily available
● Moving classrooms to cooler spaces
Procedure for Falling Below the Minimum Comfort Zone
When temperatures in the building fall below the minimum comfort zone, staff will
follow these procedures when safe, allowable, and available (but not limited to):
● Incorporate more opportunities for physical activity
● Consider having extra layers of clothing available
Procedures for Facilities Department
The Director of Buildings and Grounds, will implement measures to mitigate high
temperatures in schools.
● The Facilities Department will take inventory of air conditioning units and fans at
each school and ensure they are in good working order.
● School administrators are responsible for deciding where to place the air
conditioning units and fans within their buildings.
● The custodial staff will move the equipment as directed.
● Temperature conditions will be evaluated using a central reading at ~5 feet above
the floor in a closed zone, Consistent with ASHRAE 55. This provides an
objective and consistent standard of record, recognizing that localized hot or cold
spots may exist near windows, doors, or vents.

Public Participation At Board Meetings
DRAFT
Regular, special and emergency meetings of the School Board are open to the public. This
Board, as an elected representative body of the school unit, wishes to provide opportunity for the
public citizens to express its views interests and concerns related to the matters under
consideration by the Board. The public is invited to cordially invited to attend and participate as
set forth in this policy. Everyone in the community is invited. Board policies are available in
multiple languages on the district website.
Board meetings are conducted to carry for the purpose of carrying out the official business of the
School Department. The meetings are not public forum meetings (as are town meetings), but are
meetings which are held for the Board to do its business in public. The minutes of each public
meeting will record the action taken and will show how the Board voted on each item presented
for action. The journal of minutes is open and available to the public during normal business
hours in the Office of the Superintendent of Schools and on the district website.
Orderly conduct of a meeting does not permit spontaneous discussion from the audience nor
among Board members. Individuals or organizations desiring to make requests, presentations or
proposals on matters before the Board should make prior arrangements through the chair. will be
provided that opportunity with prior arrangements.
Generally, Opportunities for public comment participation will be provided in two places on the
agenda. The public may speak on items on or off the agenda (related to school/education) at
either or both comment periods. and may speak at one but not both times. Opportunities to speak
on agenda items will take place prior to any vote. There will be no interaction between the
public/public comment and members of the Board. the Board’s vote on those items. Comments
on items not on the agenda will take place prior to adjournment.
The intent of this policy is to allow a fair and adequate opportunity for the public to be heard, to
provide adequate time for the Board to obtain necessary information on a subject before it, and
to see that time allowed for open discussion does not interfere with the fulfillment of the
scheduled agenda of the Board.
An agenda shall be published in advance of each meeting in accordance with Board policy.
Copies are to be posted, or available at the Superintendent’s office and on the district website and
at the meeting. in each school, city hall, the public library or other appropriate public facilities.
Anyone desiring additional information about any item on the agenda should direct such
inquiries to the Office of the Superintendent.
The following “ground rules” are to further guide public comment participation at meetings:
A. All speakers will provide their name and town/city address before speaking. are to
identify themselves as they begin talking. They will not be permitted to participate in
gossip, make defamatory comments, or use abusive or vulgar language. The Board Chair

will maintain the prerogative to discontinue any presentation which violates any of the
public participation guidelines.
B. No complaints or allegations will be allowed concerning any personnel or any person
connected to the school system. If appropriate, concerns about an individual will be
handled in a conference where the rights and interests of all parties will be appropriately
regarded. Orderly conduct of a meeting does not permit spontaneous discussion from the
audience or among Board members.
C. All speakers are to address the Board Chair and may direct questions or comments to
Board Members or other officers of the school system only upon approval of the chair.
Members of the Board and the Superintendent have the privilege of asking questions of
any person who addresses the Board. Such questions must be addressed through the
chair.
D. Each speaker is given 3 minutes for comments. The Chair may limit the time given to
comments on a particular topic as well as the time any individual may speak.
E. In the event of a sizable audience,The Chair may require persons interested in speaking
to so indicate by signing up to speak, so they may be called on in the most expedient
order.
F. Comments and suggestions will be welcomed and given consideration by the Board.
Speakers may offer objective comments on school operations and programs that concern
them. Generally, the Board may hear but not discuss or act on an item not on the agenda.
Personnel and/or personal matters or complaints will not be entertained in a public
meeting but will be deferred to established resolution procedures.
G. Citizens, employees, and others with a legitimate interest in the Board’s business are
welcome to participate as provided in this policy. Others may be recognized to speak at
the Board’s discretion. Employees or employee groups will not be permitted to discuss
matters for which other, more appropriate forums, are provided.
G. Generally, duplication or repetition of comments to the Board should be avoided in
order to make the most efficient use of the time in meetings. Groups or organizations are
requested to be represented by designated spokespersons.
H. All questions will be recorded by the Superintendent and answered, to the extent
possible, at the end of public comment. If answers are not available, someone will be
assigned to reply to you as soon as the information is available.
I. The statement read at the time of Public Comment Open Discussion will be as follows:
a. The Board’s Public Comment time Open Audience of the Citizens is an
opportunity for the Board to listen to opinions of the public that relate to school
and education matters on or off this evening’s agenda. The Board is here to listen.
Anyone wishing to speak will be asked to proceed to the microphone and state
your name and town/city where you reside. All questions will be recorded by the

Superintendent and/or Chair and will be answered at the end of public comment,
to the extent possible. If answers are not available, someone will be assigned to
reply to you as soon as the answer is available. You will be given 3 minutes to
speak. There will be no interaction between the public and Board members;
additionally no clapping, booing or other interruptive behavior will be allowed.
As a reminder, School Board meetings are broadcast live, recorded and posted on
the city’s website. Please consider the impact of your comments.
