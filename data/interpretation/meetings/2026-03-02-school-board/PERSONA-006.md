# Interpretation: Tom (PERSONA-006)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop I -- March 2, 2026

### Structured Points

#### 1. The real number is 18-19%, not 6%
- **Fact:** Chair DeAngelis stated clearly that continuing all current programs and staff with no changes would require a 19% budget increase and approximately 17% property tax increase. The 6% ceiling the board set is a target requiring $7.2M in cuts -- it is not a guaranteed outcome. The chair also noted that 61% of property taxes go to education.
- **Source:** [00:05--00:06] Chair DeAngelis opening remarks
- **Emotional valence:** alarmed
- **Threat level:** high
- **Open question:** "Even if they hit the 6% target, what does that actually mean for my tax bill on a $300,000 house?"

#### 2. Enrollment dropped 300 students but staffing grew by 82
- **Fact:** The chair stated that over recent years, enrollment has declined by 300 students while staffing increased by 82 positions. This mismatch is presented as a primary driver of the budget gap. Elementary enrollment specifically dropped 23% (from 1,401 to 1,080) in four years.
- **Source:** [00:06] Chair's opening; Source 010, enrollment data
- **Emotional valence:** frustrated
- **Threat level:** moderate
- **Open question:** "How did they hire 82 people while losing 300 kids and nobody raised a flag?"

#### 3. The fund balance is gone -- there's no cushion left
- **Fact:** The chair confirmed the district has no money in reserve. The fund balance dropped from $5.9M in FY23 to essentially zero entering FY27. Previous boards used savings to hold down tax increases, effectively spending down the emergency fund.
- **Source:** [00:05] Chair's opening; FY27 budget synthesis
- **Emotional valence:** frustrated
- **Threat level:** high
- **Open question:** "So all those years they said they were keeping the increase 'reasonable,' they were just draining the savings account?"

#### 4. Per-pupil spending is the highest among comparable districts
- **Fact:** South Portland spends $26,651 per pupil -- higher than Portland ($25,891), Yarmouth ($25,451), and Gorham ($20,305). The state funds only about 20% of actual costs, despite a goal of 55%.
- **Source:** FY27 budget synthesis, citing Source 005
- **Emotional valence:** skeptical
- **Threat level:** moderate
- **Open question:** "We're spending more per kid than Portland and Yarmouth -- are we getting more per kid?"

#### 5. Reconfiguration could save $1.5-2.2M per year but the board took the biggest savings option off the table
- **Fact:** Nine elementary reconfiguration options were presented. The option with the highest 5-year savings ($19.6M, closing Skillin) was effectively removed from consideration because at least four board members publicly opposed it on equity grounds. The remaining options save less. The non-closure path (Option 1.2) scored lowest on the evaluation matrix and was never fully costed.
- **Source:** [02:06--02:11] Board discussion; Source 012, Options Matrix
- **Emotional valence:** skeptical
- **Threat level:** moderate
- **Open question:** "So they took the option that saves the most money off the table for non-financial reasons -- who pays the difference?"

#### 6. The $7.2M gap can't be closed with reconfiguration alone
- **Fact:** Even the most aggressive elementary reconfiguration (with school closure) saves $1.5-2.2M annually. Combined with middle/high school efficiencies and operational changes, total savings reach about $4M. That still leaves a $3M+ residual gap that must come from line-by-line cuts, revenue increases, or a higher tax rate.
- **Source:** Budget synthesis; [01:06--01:10] Board Q&A on residual gap
- **Emotional valence:** alarmed
- **Threat level:** high
- **Open question:** "If cutting 78 positions and closing a school doesn't close the gap, are they going to come back asking for more than 6%?"

#### 7. Board members get a $1,000 annual stipend
- **Fact:** Chair DeAngelis noted that board members receive a $1,000 annual stipend and some don't even take it. This was offered as evidence that board members are not doing this for personal gain. While not financially significant, it contextualizes that these are volunteer decision-makers.
- **Source:** [00:04--00:05] Chair's opening remarks
- **Emotional valence:** neutral
- **Threat level:** none
- **Open question:** "Fair enough -- but are they getting the right financial advice from the people they DO pay?"

### Reactions

Had to sit through another one of those school board things last night. Three and a half hours. Packed house -- mostly parents from the elementary schools. Look, the numbers are actually worse than I thought. If they don't cut anything, we're looking at a 19% increase in the school budget. Nineteen percent. The school portion is already 61% of our property taxes. They're trying to get it down to 6%, but I'll believe that when I see it.

Here's the thing that kills me -- they've lost 300 students in the last few years and somehow hired 82 more staff in the same period. The savings account is empty. They burned through almost six million dollars in reserves over three years. And now they're acting like this is some kind of surprise? The chair actually had to get up and explain that it's not a "deficit" -- they don't owe money, they just can't afford what they're paying for. Well, that's not exactly reassuring.

They showed nine different ways to reorganize the elementary schools. The one that saves the most money -- close Skillin, save about $20 million over five years -- the board killed it before the presentation was even over. Four members said they won't vote for it. I get it, it's the school with the most immigrant kids, it's a tough look. But nobody said where the extra money's coming from instead. They're going to pick a less efficient option and the difference shows up on my tax bill. Every parent who spoke last night wanted to keep things exactly the way they are. Not one of them talked about what they'd be willing to pay. They want smaller class sizes, neighborhood schools, specialist teachers, community connection -- all great. But the bill is $7 million, and it comes to us.
