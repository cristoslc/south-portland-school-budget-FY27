# Interpretation: Maria (PERSONA-001)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop I -- March 2, 2026

### Structured Points

#### 1. Her child's school might close or fundamentally change by September
- **Fact:** The administration presented nine reconfiguration options for elementary schools, several of which involve closing a school and converting the remaining four into grade-band campuses (e.g., PreK-1 primary schools and 2-4 intermediate schools). Under most options, no elementary student would stay in their current school as-is next year.
- **Source:** [00:22--01:10] Reconfiguration options presentation; Source 012, Options Matrix
- **Emotional valence:** alarmed
- **Threat level:** high
- **Open question:** "Which specific option are they actually leaning toward, and does it mean my kid changes schools in September?"

#### 2. The leadership team's top pick would close Skillin -- the school serving the most vulnerable families
- **Fact:** Option 1.7 (close Skillin, reconfigure to primary/intermediate) scored highest on the leadership's matrix at 46 points and would save $19.6M over five years. Skillin has the highest percentage of multilingual learners and free/reduced lunch students. Chair DeAngelis argued against this option on equity grounds, and at least four board members publicly stated they oppose closing Skillin.
- **Source:** [02:06--02:11] Chair DeAngelis's statement; Source 012, Matrix scores
- **Emotional valence:** frustrated
- **Threat level:** moderate
- **Open question:** "If the option that saves the most money is off the table, does that mean the rest of us pay more, or do they just pick a different school to close?"

#### 3. The board acknowledged parents haven't been directly surveyed
- **Fact:** Multiple public commenters pointed out that parents were never surveyed about reconfiguration preferences. The administration held two budget forums with exit surveys, but no targeted survey asking parents which options they could support or what trade-offs they'd accept. The board did not dispute this.
- **Source:** [02:40--02:46] Public comment section; multiple speakers
- **Emotional valence:** frustrated
- **Threat level:** moderate
- **Open question:** "How can they say they've been transparent when they never actually asked us what we think about these specific plans?"

#### 4. Transportation logistics for grade-band schools are completely unresolved
- **Fact:** Under grade-band reconfiguration, families with children in different grades would have children at different schools. No transportation cost estimates or route plans were presented. A parent noted that bus rides near Skillin already reach 45 minutes. The board asked about busing across I-295 and learned all students on the west side are already 100% bused.
- **Source:** [01:08--01:10] Board Q&A on busing; [02:44--02:45] Public comment on bus ride times
- **Emotional valence:** anxious
- **Threat level:** high
- **Open question:** "If I have a first grader and a third grader, am I doing two different drop-offs at two different schools every morning?"

#### 5. Class sizes will increase no matter what
- **Fact:** The non-reconfiguration path (Option 1.2) would still require across-the-board class size increases and staff reductions at all schools. Enrollment per grade band is projected at 200-220 students. The administration stated that even the most aggressive reconfiguration leaves a $3M+ residual gap requiring further line-by-line cuts.
- **Source:** [01:06--01:07] Board Q&A on enrollment projections; Synthesis: "Even all three together leave a $3M+ residual gap"
- **Emotional valence:** anxious
- **Threat level:** moderate
- **Open question:** "What does a bigger class actually look like for my kid -- are we talking 22 kids or 28?"

#### 6. The September timeline feels impossibly fast
- **Fact:** The administration is developing plans for September 2026 implementation. The board has three more workshops in March, a budget presentation to the city council in April, and a public referendum in June. Multiple public speakers said the pace is too fast for a change this significant.
- **Source:** [02:45--03:15] Multiple public commenters on timeline; Budget timeline from Source 011
- **Emotional valence:** alarmed
- **Threat level:** high
- **Open question:** "Are they really going to close a school and move hundreds of kids around in six months?"

#### 7. The middle school principal said mergers can work
- **Fact:** The middle school principal, Rebecca Stern, spoke about the Mahoney-Memorial merger from a few years ago. She acknowledged the anticipation was very difficult for families, students, and staff, but said the school is now "stronger together" and that the middle school community would support whatever the board decides.
- **Source:** [00:15--00:17] Principal Stern's remarks
- **Emotional valence:** cautiously_optimistic
- **Threat level:** low
- **Open question:** "But isn't merging two middle schools of older kids completely different from shuffling around kindergartners?"

### Reactions

So I finally got home from that meeting and I'm just -- I don't even know where to start. It was three and a half hours. The auditorium was packed. And basically what happened is they showed us nine different ways they could blow up the elementary schools. Nine options. Some of them close a school, some of them turn every school into just certain grades -- like one school would be PreK through first grade and another would be second through fourth. So our kids could end up changing schools every two years.

The part that made me the angriest? Their own leadership team ranked closing Skillin as the best option. Skillin -- the school with the most multilingual families, the most kids on free lunch. That's their top pick for savings. And they presented it with a straight face. At least the board chair shut that down -- she basically said four members won't vote for it, so it's off the table. But that means they're going to close a DIFFERENT school, and nobody told us which one. They didn't even have transportation cost estimates for any of these plans. Like, if my kids are at two different schools, how does that work with the bus? Nobody knows. They literally haven't figured that out yet.

And the thing that really gets me is they never asked us. They did those budget forums back in February, sure. But there was no survey, no "which of these options could you live with," nothing. A bunch of parents got up during public comment and said the same thing -- you're making the biggest change to our kids' schools in a decade and you didn't ask parents what they think. The board didn't even respond to that. They just said they'll have another workshop next week. I'm going to be there. I'm going to every single one. Because if we don't show up and keep showing up, they're going to decide this without us.
