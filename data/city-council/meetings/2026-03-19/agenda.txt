### Presentation: Pearl Street Pump Station Project
POSITION PAPER OF THE CITY MANAGER

As noted in the attached memo from Finance Director Ellen Sanborn, the City is planning to finance approximately $50 million in wastewater infrastructure improvements, including the $38 million Pearl Street Pump Station project and $12 million in plant dewatering upgrades, through the issuance of revenue bonds. Unlike general obligation bonds, revenue bonds are repaid through user fee revenues, requiring financial analysis to ensure sufficient revenue to cover debt service.

 

To support the bond issuance and obtain a favorable bond rating, the City engaged CDM Smith for a feasibility study, working alongside financial advisor Moors & Cabot and bond counsel from Pierce Atwood.

 

Preliminary analysis indicates that sewer user fees may need to increase by approximately 22% annually in FY27, FY28, and FY29 to support the bond debt service, in addition to typical operating increases of about 4% per year. For the average residential customer, the FY27 increase is estimated at about $9.70 per month ($116 annually), with an additional $11.80 per month ($141 annually) projected in FY28. However, there are options the City is looking into to mitigate the impact to sewer rate payers, include a $12 million request of our federal delegation for a federal "earmark", along with utilizing TIF revenues.

 

Final rate recommendations will depend on project bid results, interest rates, bond rating outcomes, and other sources of revenues (grants, TIF, etc.). This information will be presented to the City Council as part of the FY27 rate-setting process in June and when Council is asked to approve a revenue bond ordinance in the coming months.

 

South Portland currently maintains one of the lowest sewer rates in the Greater Portland region. Based on typical residential wastewater usage (about 4,488 gallons per month), the approximate monthly sewer costs among nearby communities are:

This places South Portland in the lower tier of regional sewer rates today, with a typical residential bill roughly 40–45% lower than Cape Elizabeth and lower than nearly all surrounding suburban systems.

 

However, the City is facing the same pressure seen across Maine as communities finance major wastewater infrastructure improvements. Recent examples in Maine include double-digit sewer rate increases in Richmond, Winthrop, and Bar Harbor, and even larger project-driven increases in Vassalboro, to name a few. South Portland’s projected increases are significant, but they reflect a broader statewide reality: aging wastewater systems - mostly built over 50 years ago in the mid-1970s - require substantial capital investment, and those costs are increasingly being borne through user rates.

 

The proposed project financing would likely move the City closer to the top of the pack over the next three fiscal years. Even so, South Portland’s projected increases are consistent with what many Maine communities are experiencing as they fund major wastewater and sewer infrastructure projects, so their rate payers are expected to see significant increases as well.

 

Finance Director Ellen Sanborn and representations from CDM Smith will provide a brief overview. Water Resource Protection Director Fred Dillon will also be present to discuss the project timeline.

### ORDER #161-25/26 - Setting the Date for the Fiscal Year 2027 Budget Public Hearing. Passage Requires Majority Vote.
POSITION PAPER OF THE CITY MANAGER

Sec. 502 of the City's charter requires the City Manager to submit the annual budget proposal no later than the third Monday in March. This year that is March 16, 2026. In addition, Sec. 504 of the charter requires the Council to set a public hearing on the budget at the meeting where the budget and budget message are submitted. Since the Council does not meet on Mondays, this means that Council must vote on a hearing date at its meeting on March 19, 2026. Traditionally, Council has set the hearing for its first meeting in April. As such, the attached Order would set April 7, 2026, at the public hearing date for the FY27 budget.

### ORDER #164-25/26 - Approving Sale of Land Identified on Tax Assessor's Map 26 as parts of Lots 16B and 16G at Elm St & Forest Ave. Passage requires majority vote.
POSITION PAPER OF THE CITY MANAGER

In March 2023, the City Council approved the purchase of two small parcels of land in the Pleasantdale neighborhood located at the intersection of Elm Street and Forest Avenue. The City had owned the small strip of land bisecting both parcels, which had previously served as a rail line to the shipyards on the eastern waterfront during World War II.

 

With adjacent parcels now under common City ownership, the plan is to create a small park and open space in the neighborhood. As part of that process, there is a need to address a known boundary issue with the neighboring lot at 100 Elm Street, where the driveway and side yard encroach onto City land. City staff have worked with the owners of 100 Elm Street on a solution to square off the parcel for a modest fee. This is essentially a "clean up" of lot lines that resulted from a legacy industrial activity. Please see the attached memo from Assistant City Manager Joshua Reny for more details. The draft Purchase & Sale Agreement has been reviewed by Corporation Counsel.

 

Council is being asked to approve the sale of land as specified on the attached order to achieve this boundary line clean up.

 

Asst. Manager Reny will be present should Council remove this item from the Consent Calendar.

### Discussion About City Facilities/Mahoney Project
POSITION PAPER OF THE CITY MANAGER

On February 17, 2026, the City Council provided guidance to City staff and the design team to provide a cost estimate for a revised project at the Mahoney property which blended presented Option A1 as well as some elements included in Option B1, such as geothermal technology to reduce energy use, and number of elevators. This project would create a city service center at Mahoney and enable public use of the theater and current gym space for community events, but would not include the library and would essential leave the third floor vacant.

 

As will be discussed further at the meeting, and as detailed in the attached document, the City's design team estimates even with this reduced scope, the total project will cost $70 - $76 million. The design team will also present various options for how the building space could be utilized.

 

It is estimated that a new combined City Hall could be built for just under half this amount, around $38-$45 million.

 

Council is being asked to provide guidance on the following:

 

- Does Council wish to proceed with the scaled down project as proposed, or does Council wish to look at other locations for a City Hall?
- Is the Council interested in engaging with a developer via an RFP process to see if a public/private partnership at Mahoney is feasible?
 

If Council proceeds with the plan as proposed, it would be ready for a November 2026 bond ask of voters, which could run concurrent with the bond ask for the public safety facilities. If Council wishes to locate a new site and/or explore what a public/private partnership might look like, a referendum would need to be pushed to at least November 2027. NOTE: if maintaining the theater and/or gym for community use is important to the Council, the Mahoney property could be sold to a developer with the proviso that one or both spaces be maintained in some fashion for public use.

 

3. How does Council wish to address facility needs at the Library?

 

Council current guidance is to not include the Library as part of the renovations project, although at Council's last meeting some Councilors indicated a willingness to revisit this topic. If Library is not part of a project, there will be investments the City will need to make into the current facility. Is Council interested in a bond request to make those renovations. Or, does the Council wish to proceed with a full renovation of the Library on site or at another location?

 

Facilities Director Don Hopkins, Library Director Kevin Davis, and representatives from the City's design team will be present for this discussion.

### ORDER #168-25/26 - Authorizing the City Manager to Enter Into a Purchase and Sale Agreement for the 279 Main Street Property and Appropriate Funds to Execute the Transaction. Passage Requires Majority Vote.
POSITION PAPER OF THE CITY MANAGER

In early February, the City Council directed the City Manager in executive session to make an offer to purchase the property at 279 Main Street, where Walgreens is currently located. Walgreens had announced it was closing, and the property was listed for $2.85 million.

 

Working with broker Tom Moulton of The Dunham Group, the City submitted an offer of $2.525 million, which was accepted. The City is currently in a 40-day due diligence period to confirm the site is suitable for the construction of a new Police Station. Preliminary findings indicate that it is.

 

Council is being asked to authorize the City Manager to enter into a formal purchase and sale agreement so that the City can eventually close on this sale if everything turns up satisfactory during the review period.

 

No property tax increase or budget impact will be required to complete the Walgreens property purchase. The City plans to use $1.5 million in Police Asset Forfeiture funds - which can only be spent for Police purposes - and $1 million in TIF funds, both previously earmarked for city facilities. Voter approval will still be required to construct a Police Station at this location, which would replace the current Walgreens building. The Council previously provided guidance to staff at a March 3, 2026 workshop discussion to pursue a public safety bond referendum before voters this November, which would include the new Police Station and rebuilding the Central Fire Station on site. The City’s design team and staff are currently working to determine the estimated construction cost.

 

Previously, a new Police Station had been proposed as part of the larger Mahoney City Center project, which included a combined City Hall, Hamlin Building, and Main Library. The initial City Center plan included a renovated theater, gym and community space in the Mahoney building, a new Police Station on the Mahoney field, and a new Central Fire Station on its existing site. Due largely to the $194 million estimated cost, the Council chose not to pursue that proposal and instead reassessed the City’s facility plans.
